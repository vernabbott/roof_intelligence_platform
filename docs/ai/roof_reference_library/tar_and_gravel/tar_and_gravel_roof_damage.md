# Tar and Gravel Roof Damage Identification

## Description

This file provides a starting reference for identifying and documenting visible damage on tar-and-gravel built-up roof (BUR) surfaces. These systems generally consist of multiple asphalt or coal-tar-saturated plies covered by a flood coat and aggregate surfacing. Because gravel often conceals the waterproofing layers, observations should include aggregate coverage and displacement as well as visible evidence of exposed bitumen or felts, cracking, blistering, splitting, ponding, flashing failure, and moisture-related deformation.

## Why It's Important

The aggregate surfacing protects the bituminous membrane from ultraviolet exposure, impact, fire, and temperature extremes. Bare spots, displaced gravel, exposed felts, splits, and blisters can accelerate deterioration or allow water into the roof assembly. Since damage and trapped moisture may be hidden beneath aggregate, recognizing indirect warning signs is important for preventing leaks, planning repairs, assessing drainage, and estimating remaining service life.

## Visual Characteristics

### Aging

- Bare, thin, scoured, embedded, or displaced gravel exposing the flood coat
- Brittle asphalt, alligatoring, surface erosion, bleed-out, and exposed felts
- Widespread sediment, vegetation, and deterioration of protective surfacing
- Cracked roof cement, patches, coatings, and flashing materials

### Ponding

- Standing water between aggregate or in visible low areas
- Dark wet gravel, staining, sediment rings, algae, and vegetation
- Gravel and debris blocking drains, scuppers, gutters, or drainage paths
- Depressions, settlement, and soft-looking areas that may indicate trapped moisture

### Leaking

- Exposed felts, open cracks, splits, punctures, and failed flashings
- Deteriorated repairs or roof cement around drains, walls, curbs, and penetrations
- Sunken, soft, swollen, or irregular areas that may indicate saturated insulation
- Staining and aggregate disturbance concentrated near a suspected entry point

### Hail Damage

- Freshly displaced gravel and circular impact patterns in exposed flood coat
- Bruised, fractured, punctured, or crushed membrane beneath thin aggregate
- Chipped or broken surfacing and impact damage to metal flashings and equipment
- Concentrated bare spots or exposed felts following severe hail

### Deformation

- Blisters, bubbles, ridges, buckles, wrinkles, or raised membrane areas
- Gravel piled along ridges or displaced around moving membrane
- Depressions, settlement, and uneven areas associated with wet insulation or deck movement
- Asphalt slippage, flow, or membrane displacement on sloped areas

### Splitting

- Linear cracks, fractures, or open splits visible through displaced gravel
- Splits aligned with insulation joints, structural movement, ridges, and transitions
- Cracked flashings, patches, roof cement, and perimeter details
- Openings that expose reinforcement felts, insulation, or substrate

## AI Confidence Factors

AI confidence should account for the membrane being partly or completely hidden by aggregate and for normal variations in gravel color and depth.

### High Confidence

- Bare membrane, exposed felts, ponding, major splits, or failed flashing is clearly visible
- Gravel displacement or drainage blockage has a distinct and consistent pattern
- Multiple images confirm the condition and affected area
- Image resolution distinguishes aggregate, debris, vegetation, water, and exposed bitumen
- Supporting signs such as staining, ridging, or material loss accompany the suspected defect

### Medium Confidence

- Gravel coverage appears thin, uneven, or disturbed, but membrane exposure is uncertain
- A raised, dark, or depressed area may indicate blistering, moisture, ponding, or normal aggregate variation
- Roof texture, shadows, wet gravel, or oblique perspective affects interpretation
- Only one image or viewing angle is available

### Low Confidence

- The suspected membrane defect is hidden beneath gravel
- Dark areas could be wet aggregate, exposed bitumen, staining, or shadow
- Apparent ridges or depressions may result from image perspective or normal gravel placement
- The roof system, material depth, or affected component cannot be confirmed

Low- and medium-confidence findings should be verified through closer imagery and an on-site inspection that includes safe aggregate displacement and moisture testing where appropriate.

## Severity

Severity should consider aggregate coverage, ply exposure, splitting or blistering, drainage performance, flashing condition, and evidence of trapped moisture or active leakage.

### Minor

Localized aggregate variation, light debris, or superficial weathering with no exposed felts, significant cracking, drainage restriction, or moisture indicator. Monitor during routine inspection.

### Moderate

Localized bare spots, developing cracks, minor blistering, deteriorated roof cement, vegetation, or partial drainage restriction that may accelerate deterioration. Schedule aggregate redistribution, cleaning, and professional repair evaluation.

### Severe

Exposed felts, major splits, widespread bare areas, large blisters, failed flashing, blocked primary drainage, or deformation indicating possible wet insulation. Prioritize prompt repair and moisture assessment.

### Critical

Extensive membrane or ply failure, active water intrusion, widespread saturated or unstable areas, major drainage failure, or loss of immediate weather protection. Escalate for urgent professional assessment and temporary protection.
