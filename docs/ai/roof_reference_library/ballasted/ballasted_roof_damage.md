# Ballasted Roof Damage Identification

## Description

This file provides a starting reference for identifying and documenting visible damage on ballasted low-slope roof systems. These roofs commonly use loose river-washed stone or concrete pavers to hold an underlying single-ply membrane and insulation assembly in place. Because the membrane is often concealed, observations should address both the condition and distribution of the ballast and any visible evidence of damage to the membrane, flashings, drainage components, or roof-mounted equipment.

## Why It's Important

Ballast protects the membrane from ultraviolet exposure, temperature changes, and wind uplift. Displaced, sparse, piled, or contaminated ballast can leave the membrane vulnerable or impose uneven loads on the roof assembly. Hidden punctures, seam failures, and trapped moisture may not be directly visible, so accurate recognition of indirect warning signs is important for inspection planning, leak prevention, wind-performance assessment, and safe maintenance.

## Visual Characteristics

### Aging

- Weathered, dirty, or embedded ballast with widespread sediment and organic growth
- Brittle, chalked, cracked, or discolored membrane visible in bare areas
- Deteriorated flashings, sealants, patches, and edge components
- Repeated ballast displacement or thinning that leaves the membrane exposed

### Ponding

- Standing water visible between stones or over exposed membrane
- Dark wet aggregate, algae, sediment rings, or vegetation in persistent low areas
- Ballast migration into drain bowls, scuppers, and drainage paths
- Depressions, uneven settlement, or water accumulation near equipment and curbs

### Leaking

- Open membrane seams, punctures, or failed flashings visible beneath displaced ballast
- Staining, sediment trails, or vegetation concentrated around a suspected entry point
- Soft, sunken, or irregular roof areas that may indicate wet insulation
- Repeated patching or disturbed ballast around penetrations, drains, and transitions

### Hail Damage

- Freshly displaced or scattered ballast following a storm
- Cracked, chipped, or broken concrete pavers
- Punctures, dents, bruises, or impact marks on exposed membrane and metal flashings
- Concentrated impact patterns on rooftop equipment, vents, and exposed accessories

### Deformation

- Ballast piled into ridges, drifts, or unusually deep concentrations
- Wrinkles, ridges, buckling, bubbling, or bridging in exposed membrane
- Rocking, shifted, sunken, or misaligned pavers
- Uneven settlement, depressions, or raised areas associated with deck or insulation movement

### Splitting

- Linear openings, tears, or cracks in exposed membrane
- Separation at seams, transitions, penetrations, and perimeter flashings
- Cracked patches or repairs with lifted edges
- Splits aligned with underlying insulation joints, structural movement, or concentrated stress

## AI Confidence Factors

AI confidence should account for the fact that ballast may conceal the waterproofing membrane.

### High Confidence

- Ballast displacement, exposed membrane, broken pavers, or ponding is clearly visible
- The affected area has a distinct boundary and adequate surrounding roof context
- Multiple images confirm the same distribution pattern or defect
- A visible membrane opening, failed flashing, or blocked drain can be directly observed
- Image scale and resolution are sufficient to distinguish ballast from debris or shadow

### Medium Confidence

- Ballast appears uneven or sparse, but depth and membrane exposure are uncertain
- Suspected ponding, settlement, or vegetation is partially obscured by stone
- The condition is visible in only one image or at an oblique angle
- Lighting, roof slope, or image perspective may exaggerate distribution differences

### Low Confidence

- The suspected defect is distant, blurry, heavily shadowed, or hidden by ballast
- Dark areas could be wet stone, membrane exposure, staining, or shadow
- Normal variations in stone color or placement could resemble damage
- Membrane condition is inferred without visible supporting evidence

Low- and medium-confidence findings should be verified through closer imagery and an on-site inspection that safely moves ballast where appropriate.

## Severity

Severity should consider ballast coverage, membrane exposure, drainage, wind-uplift risk, loading, and evidence of water intrusion.

### Minor

Small, localized ballast variation, light debris, or isolated paver wear with no visible membrane exposure, drainage restriction, or active leak indicator. Monitor during routine inspection.

### Moderate

Localized thin coverage, displaced ballast, minor paver movement, vegetation, sediment buildup, or drainage interference that could reduce protection or accelerate deterioration. Schedule redistribution, cleaning, and professional evaluation.

### Severe

Large exposed membrane areas, significant wind scour, broken or missing pavers, blocked primary drainage, visible membrane damage, or widespread ballast migration. Prioritize prompt professional inspection and corrective work.

### Critical

Extensive membrane exposure or failure, major ballast displacement after a storm, dangerous loose materials, overloaded or unstable areas, active water intrusion, or drainage failure that threatens the roof assembly or building safety. Escalate for urgent assessment and temporary protection.
